Why this exists

Your HTS classifications were correct in January. By April, they were costing you.

In 2025, the same product could carry 28% in additional duties one quarter and face a refund claim the next. Your ERP did not update automatically. Your broker caught it weeks later, on the invoice. That is not a technology failure. That is what happens when tariff intelligence lives in spreadsheets while trade policy moves at the speed of executive orders.

In February 2026, the Supreme Court invalidated IEEPA tariffs — the legal basis for most of the duties imposed since early 2025. Congress responded with Section 122, a 10% global surcharge that took effect February 24, 2026. It expires July 24, 2026 unless extended. New Section 301 investigations targeting 16 countries launched in March. The CAPE refund portal opened in April for importers who overpaid during the IEEPA period. Every one of these events changed landed costs for active importers.

TariffSense tracks this in real time. Classification, costing, FTA analysis, and scenario modeling — the work that currently lives across tariff books, broker portals, and Excel — in one platform, with tariff intelligence that refreshes automatically every two weeks.
